High School Projects

My real interest in programming started back in 8th grade. I had QuickBasic 4.5 installed on our family's computer and with the help of a few books I checked out from the library I started teaching myself how to program. I think I enjoy programming so much (and software development in general) because I have always enjoyed building things -- mechanical things, models, experiments, whatever. I also enjoyed art classes and other forms of self-expression. Math and Science have also been favorite subjects. Programming has always been a way for me to combine all the things I like into one.

Medieval Massacre (Spring 1994)

Medieval Massacre is a multiplayer (one computer) graphical game written in QuickBasic. It was the first large project I ever completed. It showed a map of a kingdom and the goal was to conquer other player's castles. As soon as their last castle was destroyed they were eliminated from the game. It was an excellent experience. It really got me excited about programming. The difficulty in maintaining the code and the style I used in writing the game have convinced me of the benefits of object oriented programming. The project became huge - it exceeded 5,000 lines of code. Because of its size and the way it was written it would be a nightmare to try and go back and understand it all now.

PLD Editor (Summer 1995)

My best friend and I had decided to create a role playing game. He worked on the story and helped a little bit with programming, while I figured out how to do the programming part. The game would use tiled images as the basis for the graphics. To help with the creation of these tiles I created an editor (which used the mouse and wasn't too common for QuickBasic programs) which could work with a set of tiles. These sets of tiles could also be associated with multiple palettes. I created commands which you could click on to perform various functions - including merging images and shifting the palettes. There were also nice features to edit the palettes and customize the colors, as well as blend palette colors together over a range of colors. The real beauty that I saw was the ability to create one tile and then with a careful selection of colors shift the palettes to make it appear that the seasons were changing. I added the ability to animate different tiled images also.

World Builder (Summer 1995)

The world builder was an editor which helped to create the world for the game. The game world was huge so a nice world buider was necessary. The world builder drew the screen in memory from the tiles and then allowed you to scroll around the world, refershing tiles when necessary. Some of the nice features were the ability to smooth mountains together and drag and draw roads and shorelines. There were also features to zoom out to different levels and then zoom-in at different locations.

Bridge Builder (Summer 1996)

My best friend and I were excited about the bridge building contest at Utah State University but we ran into some difficulties in designing our bridges. We had some basic design rules in mind, but we weren't sure how to optimize our design. I wrote a program which would show relative beam strengths pased upon the parameters which were input, and then it was simply a matter of re-arranging the input (and the structural design) to maximize the strength of the weakest beam and try to make it so all beams had equal strength. We were happy to build some bridges as high school students which surpassed in strength the bridges built by the engineering students on campus. My friend won the contest the next spring with a bridge holding 2500 lbs, the next closest bridge holding somewhere around 1800-1900 lbs.

Salisbury Photo Accounting Software (Summer 1997)

My father runs his own Photography business and to make his life simpler he wanted me to write a program for him to record his account information. He wanted the ability to group income and expense items into hierarchies and then generate reports that he could use for his own interest and for tax purposes. I was quite happy with the program because of the speed in which I developed it and the use my father got out of the program. Later on I discovered that Quicken had much the same functionality as the program I had written. I also discovered once I was in college that it would have been much easier to create windowed applications with better development tools than the ones I had used (like Visual C++ or Visual Basic).
